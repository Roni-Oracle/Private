# Write an LI-CBT Clinical Case

## Objective

Create a focused, professional, intelligent, readable clinical case that reflects the treatment actually delivered, satisfies the course rubric, protects the patient, and fits 5–8 final pages without padding or clinically important omissions.

Write in Hebrew and RTL unless the user requests another language. Prefer a polished `.docx` when the user asks for a document; otherwise provide finished structured text.

## Required references

Read these files before drafting:

- `references/clinical-requirements.md` for non-negotiable clinical and rubric requirements.
- `references/compression-and-structure.md` for the 5–8-page architecture and selection rules.
- `references/lecturer-review-rubric.md` for the two quality-control passes.

Read `references/unified-guide.md` when any requirement, tool, sequencing decision, problem-specific module, or rubric interpretation is uncertain. It is the complete source guide. For large-file navigation, search it with terms such as `מסוכנות|4W|אמפתיה|יומן 1|יומן 2|יומן 3|חץ אנכי|המשגה|חשיפה|מניעת נפילה|הדרכה|רפלקציה`.

## Operating rules

1. Never invent patient facts, quotations, scores, dates, sessions, interventions, outcomes, consent, supervision, or clinical decisions.
2. Separate three layers: documented fact, patient report, and clinical inference. Phrase uncertainty explicitly.
3. Treat the course rubric as the highest-priority source, followed by explicit instructor guidance, the long-case/portfolio templates, and then course materials.
4. Use a working hypothesis rather than a formal diagnosis when the writer's qualification or evidence does not support diagnosis.
5. Protect confidentiality. Remove names, exact dates, locations, workplaces, institutions, contact details, signatures, rare combinations, and identifying metadata.
6. Escalate risk, scope, diagnosis, medication, mandatory-reporting, and legal questions to current supervision and organizational policy. Do not turn the case report into clinical or legal authorization.
7. Include only tools that were actually used. If a rubric-required tool is missing, identify the gap; do not write as though it occurred.
8. Show what happened in treatment, not only textbook explanations of CBT.
9. Preserve the patient's voice with brief anonymized quotations and preserve the therapist's reasoning with explicit decision points.
10. Do not expose private chain-of-thought. Report conclusions, evidence, revisions, and remaining gaps concisely.
11. Do not create bibliography entries from memory. Use references present in the supplied materials or verify exact bibliographic details in an authoritative source; otherwise state that the source details require completion.

## Workflow

### 1. Inventory and extract the evidence

Inspect all supplied files and identify:

- case-setting, consent, anonymization, referral, and treatment status;
- relevant background, history, strengths, supports, health, medication, and prior care;
- presenting complaint in the patient's words, 4W, FFF, impairment, triggers, avoidance, and safety behaviors;
- risk assessments, PHQ-9 question 9 follow-up, scope, differential considerations, and supervision;
- all measures by session, including PHQ-9, GAD-7, problem-specific scales, and subjective ratings;
- individualized maintenance cycle, formulation, goal, and treatment plan;
- session chronology, psychoeducation, tools, worksheets, homework, quotations, resistance, and clinical decisions;
- outcomes, setbacks, termination, relapse prevention, follow-up, supervision, and student reflection.

Build an internal evidence ledger with fields: `claim`, `source`, `session`, `patient words/data`, `clinical meaning`, `confidence`, `include?`. Do not place this ledger in the final report unless requested.

### 2. Resolve gaps before writing

Classify missing information:

- **Blocking:** risk/suicidality, scope/suitability, consent/setting, baseline or outcome measures required by the rubric, whether a quoted intervention actually occurred, or treatment status. Ask focused questions before finalizing.
- **Important but non-blocking:** exact session number, minor background detail, or secondary technique. Draft using an explicit neutral limitation such as `לא תועד בחומר שנמסר`, if clinically appropriate.
- **Nonessential:** omit it.

Ask only questions that materially change safety, accuracy, rubric compliance, or the selected clinical narrative.

### 3. Choose the clinical thesis

Define one sentence that explains the case:

`הקושי המרכזי נשמר באמצעות ___; לכן הטיפול התמקד ב___; השינוי נבחן באמצעות ___.`

Use this thesis to decide what deserves space. Prefer evidence that advances formulation, intervention, decision, or outcome.

### 4. Plan the 5–8 pages before drafting

Use `references/compression-and-structure.md`. Aim for 6–7 pages so final rendering has room to vary.

Default text target when actual pagination is unavailable: approximately 2,500–3,600 Hebrew words, including headings and bibliography. Treat this only as an estimate; actual pagination controls.

Do not reach the page limit by shrinking below 10.5–11 pt, using very narrow margins, or compressing line spacing unnaturally. Shorten content instead.

### 5. Draft a coherent clinical narrative

Write in this order unless the case requires a justified variation:

1. Title, anonymization statement, case snapshot, setting, and treatment status.
2. Focused intake, presenting complaint, 4W/FFF, functioning, strengths, risk, scope, and measures.
3. individualized maintenance cycle, formulation, focused emotional/behavioral goal, and plan.
4. treatment course organized by meaningful phases and turning points rather than a diary of every meeting.
5. selected cognitive and behavioral work with actual evidence, including the five empathy principles.
6. measurement trend, obstacles, supervision, results, termination, relapse prevention, and reflection.
7. concise bibliography.

For every selected intervention, use the evidence chain:

`when and why → therapist wording/action → patient response → artifact/rating → result → next decision`.

Make the text interesting through clinical specificity, patient voice, dilemmas, turning points, and reflective honesty. Do not use dramatization, praise, or generic textbook prose as a substitute for evidence.

### 6. Represent the required tools compactly

Keep the correct sequence and course logic. Cover only tools actually used, while flagging rubric gaps. Use compact examples rather than full forms:

- one precise example each from diaries 1, 2, and 3;
- one meaningful Socratic dialogue, labeled by question function when useful;
- the downward-arrow sequence and evidence that the endpoint is a core belief;
- a compact formulation connecting past and present cases;
- the avoidant/safety-behavior table before exposure work;
- core-belief challenge across two sessions and the behavioral experiment between them, when performed;
- exposure/activation/problem-solving evidence with prediction, action, result, and learning;
- a compact five-row empathy table containing one short dialogue excerpt per principle, its function, and the patient's response.

### 7. Run the CBT expert review

Adopt the stance of an experienced CBT clinician. Review against `references/lecturer-review-rubric.md` and revise for:

- factual and clinical accuracy;
- formulation–goal–intervention–outcome coherence;
- safety, scope, ethics, and non-fabrication;
- collaborative stance, empathy, and patient voice;
- appropriate sequencing and rationale;
- measurement-based decisions and honest treatment limits.

### 8. Run the LI-CBT lecturer review

Adopt the stance of a qualified lecturer grading a long LI-CBT case. Identify likely lost marks, then revise for:

- explicit rubric coverage rather than implied coverage;
- correct examples and timing of course tools;
- concise evidence for every important claim;
- intelligent reflection and use of supervision;
- readability, structure, professional Hebrew, and a clear clinical arc;
- a final length of 5–8 pages.

Do not merely score the draft. Apply the revisions.

### 9. Compress with clinical judgment

Delete in this order:

1. duplicated explanations and repeated conclusions;
2. generic CBT/DSM teaching not individualized to the patient;
3. background details that do not affect formulation, risk, treatment, or outcome;
4. multiple examples proving the same point—retain the strongest;
5. full transcripts and full worksheets—retain decisive excerpts;
6. session-by-session logistics—retain milestones and turning points;
7. weak adjectives, meta-commentary, and defensive justifications.

Never cut safety/scope, the presenting complaint and 4W, measure trends, formulation, goal, core intervention evidence, five empathy principles, outcome, relapse prevention, supervision, or reflection merely to meet length.

### 10. Verify length and deliver

Run:

```text
python scripts/audit_case.py <draft.md-or-docx>
```

If creating a Word document, use the available document-generation skill and render the `.docx` to confirm the actual page count and RTL layout. Iterate until the final body, including references unless the course says otherwise, is 5–8 pages. Keep optional raw forms in a separate anonymized appendix only if permitted.

Deliver:

- the final case text or `.docx`;
- actual page count when rendered, otherwise word count plus estimated range;
- a brief quality note listing the two review passes completed;
- only unresolved material gaps or supervision issues that remain clinically important.

Do not burden the user with the internal evidence ledger, every rejected detail, or a long audit report unless requested.
