# Clinical and rubric requirements

## Priority and safety gates

Use this order of authority:

1. Grading rubric and explicit instructor directions.
2. Long-case and portfolio templates.
3. Course protocol and course worksheets.
4. The short-case example for presentation quality only.

Do not finalize when any of these is unknown or falsely implied:

- informed consent, student status, setting, confidentiality, and anonymization;
- current/past suicidality or harm risk, PHQ-9 question 9 follow-up, and action taken;
- suitability for LI-CBT and escalation/referral decisions;
- actual measures, scores, dates/session numbers, and treatment status;
- whether a quoted conversation or intervention occurred.

## Evidence standard

For every major tool or clinical decision, document:

1. session/phase and trigger for using it;
2. rationale and how it was explained;
3. exact therapist wording or compact process description;
4. patient response, quotation, rating, or completed artifact;
5. immediate result and later follow-up;
6. the next clinical decision;
7. supervision or reflection when relevant.

Prefer one strong, traceable example to several vague statements.

## Required case content

### Setting and intake

- Student identity and limits, supervision, consent, confidentiality, contact/cancellation rules, and treatment status.
- Clinically relevant age/life context, family/development, health, medication, prior treatment, substances, functioning, strengths, support, and why now.
- Presenting complaint in the patient's words.
- A single precise 4W situation: what, where, when, and who.
- Thoughts, emotions and intensity, body, behavior, FFF, triggers, impairment, development, maintaining factors, and prior coping.

### Assessment and suitability

- Symptom clusters with examples, duration, frequency, severity, and functional effect.
- Differential considerations and medical/substance/medication alternatives.
- Working hypothesis stated within the student's authority.
- LI-CBT suitability and reasons for referral, parallel care, or increased intensity.
- Direct, documented risk assessment. Follow up any non-zero PHQ-9 question 9 response according to current policy and supervision.

### Measurement

- PHQ-9 and GAD-7 at baseline and continuously according to rubric/course protocol.
- For social anxiety, Liebowitz monitoring approximately every 3–5 sessions when required by the rubric.
- Problem-specific and subjective goal ratings.
- One compact trend graph/table or narrative explaining improvement, spikes, non-response, and the resulting decisions.

### Psychoeducation, motivation, and goal

- State what was actually explained about CBT and the problem.
- Show the patient's individualized maintenance cycle, including avoidance and safety behaviors.
- Explain the rationale at transitions between tools.
- Address outcome and process resistance.
- If used, document the magic button, paradoxical cost-benefit, magic dial / חוגת הקסמים, and time elevator with the patient's response.
- State the goal first in the patient's words, then as an observable emotional/behavioral target: reduce emotion from X to adaptive Y in a precise situation/audience. Do not target zero when the emotion has adaptive value.

### Cognitive sequence

- Diary 1: objective event, automatic thought, emotion/intensity, body, and FFF behavior.
- Diary 2: hot thought, distortions, evidence, balanced alternative, belief and emotional change.
- Socratic questioning: show actual therapist questions and patient answers; include clarification, assumptions, evidence, consequences, perspectives, and metacognitive questioning when relevant.
- Downward arrow: start from an automatic thought and show the sequence to an identity-level core belief. Explain how it was validated across events and with the patient.
- Formulation: relevant early experiences, core belief, assumptions, rules, biased expectations, coping/safety strategies, strengths, and two cases tied to the same belief—one past and one current.
- Avoidance/safety table: present after diary 2 and before exposure planning; connect its items to the chosen behavioral work.
- Diary 3: one specific event, dominant emotion, hot thought, distortions, alternatives, belief re-ratings, and emotional re-rating. If the course expects a fully credible alternative and it was below 100%, describe the honest process used to examine the remaining doubt rather than inventing a score.
- Core-belief challenge: place after diary 3, show work across two sessions, and include the behavioral experiment planned between them when performed.

### Behavioral work

- Every homework task must specify what, when, where, with whom, duration/frequency, prediction, planned reduction of safety behavior, recording method, and review.
- Exposure: individualized rationale, consent, hierarchy, prediction, SUDS/learning measures, safety behaviors to reduce, execution, actual outcome, learning, repetition, and next step. Follow current supervision for medical safety, interoceptive exposure, trauma, and course-specific habituation rules.
- Behavioral activation: prior valued activities, activity schedule, graded steps, predicted versus actual difficulty/mastery/pleasure, and effect on mood/function.
- Problem solving: one controllable problem, brainstorm, short/long-term costs and gains, patient choice, rehearsal, implementation, and review.
- Relaxation/regulation: technique, timing, purpose, in-session practice, home use, effect, adoption or alternative, and whether it risks becoming a safety behavior.

### Five empathy principles

Include one brief anonymized dialogue excerpt for each:

1. agreement/disarming—validate the true or understandable part without endorsing harm or distortion;
2. thought-and-feeling reflection—check accuracy with the patient;
3. I Feel—brief, bounded, patient-serving therapist feeling;
4. stroking/encouragement—specific authentic recognition of effort, value, or courage;
5. inquiry/invitation to change—an open, non-coercive invitation to a small next step.

For each, give context, therapist line, patient response, clinical function, and observed effect. A five-row table is the most space-efficient format.

### Ending and professional learning

- Compare baseline, middle, and end measures with functional and behavioral evidence.
- State fully, partly, and unmet goals; avoid attributing all change to therapy.
- Summarize adopted and rejected tools, patient feedback, and next goals.
- Show relapse-prevention work begun before the final session: warning signs, high-risk situations, actions, supports, return-to-care criteria, and 3-/6-month goals.
- Show supervision questions, recommendations, implementation, and learning.
- Include honest clinical and personal reflection, limits, suitability in retrospect, and what the student would change.
- Include concise, accurate references for the protocol, diagnostic framework, and central tools actually used.

## Problem-specific checks

Load the relevant module only:

- **Social anxiety:** Liebowitz trend, self-focused attention, pre/post-event processing, safety behaviors, specific social predictions, hierarchy, and exposure learning.
- **GAD:** worry topics, useful versus unproductive worry, control circle, beliefs about worry, uncertainty exposure, problem solving, and worry-time/monitoring if used.
- **Panic/agoraphobia:** panic diary, catastrophic bodily interpretation, medical screening, interoceptive and in-vivo exposure, safety-behavior reduction, and fear-of-fear change.
- **Depression:** PHQ-9/Q9, scope, activity monitoring, values-based activation, graded tasks, mastery/pleasure, and parallel risk monitoring.
- **Health anxiety:** adequate medical assessment, checking/reassurance/information seeking or avoidance, maintenance cycle, and safe reduction of reassurance behaviors.
- **Procrastination/perfectionism:** function of avoidance, underlying rules/beliefs, small timed steps, predicted/actual difficulty, sufficient-quality experiments, and self-worth decoupled from performance.
- **Anger/assertiveness:** specific ABC/FFF episode, early signals, passive/aggressive/assertive distinction, rehearsal, problem solving, and safety planning when relevant.
- **Adolescents:** assent/parent consent, confidentiality boundaries, developmentally adapted language, parent role, school/peers/digital context, and risk procedure.
- **OCD/PTSD/major complexity:** educational course content does not expand the student's scope; document supervision and referral unless specially trained and authorized.
